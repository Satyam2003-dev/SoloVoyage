Food & Drink Safety

Experiencing local cuisine is a highlight of travel. Follow these guidelines to ensure your culinary adventures are memorable for the right reasons.

Eat at Busy Restaurants

High customer turnover usually indicates fresh food and reduces your risk of foodborne illness.

  • Follow the Locals: A restaurant that is consistently busy with local patrons (not just tourists) is a strong indicator of quality and safety.
  • Signs of Freshness: High turnover means ingredients are being used quickly and not sitting around for long periods, which is key to avoiding food poisoning.
  • Observe Cleanliness: Take a quick look at the general hygiene of the establishmentâ€”clean tables, floors, and staff are all positive signs. If a place looks dirty, trust your gut and leave.

Be Cautious with Street Food

Street food can be delicious and safe, but it requires careful selection to avoid illness.

  • Made to Order: Choose vendors who cook the food fresh right in front of you. Ensure it is served piping hot, as high temperatures kill most harmful bacteria.
  • Avoid Risky Items: Be wary of food that has been sitting out for a long time, raw or uncooked items, pre-cut fruits, and anything containing un-refrigerated dairy or mayonnaise.
  • Hygiene Check: Observe the vendor's practices. Do they handle money and food with the same hands? Is their cart and preparation area clean? Choose vendors with high standards.

Drink Bottled Water When Necessary

In areas with questionable water quality, sealed bottled water is your best defense against serious illness.

  • Check the Seal: Always ensure the cap on a bottle of water is factory-sealed. Some vendors in tourist areas are known to refill old bottles with tap water.
  • Avoid Ice and Raw Veggies: Say no to ice in your drinks and be cautious of raw salads, as both are often prepared with local tap water.
  • Use for Brushing Teeth: If the tap water is not safe to drink, it is also not safe for brushing your teeth. Use bottled water for this purpose as well.

Watch How Food is Prepared

Observing food preparation helps you identify potential health risks before you eat.

  • Look for Good Practices: Watch for clean cooking surfaces, separate utensils for raw and cooked foods, and staff who wash their hands frequently.
  • Red Flags: Be cautious if you see a cook using the same knife on raw meat and then on vegetables, if food is stored at room temperature, or if there are flies around the preparation area.
  • Open Kitchens are a Good Sign: Restaurants and stalls with open kitchens that are proud to show their preparation methods are often a safer bet.

Eat Fruits You Can Peel Yourself

Fruits with a thick, removable peel significantly reduce your risk of consuming contaminated water or bacteria.

  • Safe Choices: Bananas, oranges, mangoes, pineapples, and avocados are excellent choices because you remove the outer layer that may have been contaminated.
  • Fruits to Avoid: Be cautious with fruits that you eat whole, like berries, grapes, or apples, unless you can wash them yourself thoroughly with purified water.
  • Avoid Pre-Cut Fruit: Decline pre-cut fruit salads from street vendors or buffets, as you don't know when they were cut or how they were washed.

Limit Alcohol Consumption

Excessive drinking impairs your judgment and makes you vulnerable to crime, accidents, and scams.

  • Know Your Travel Limits: Your usual alcohol tolerance can be significantly lower when you are affected by jet lag, dehydration, or altitude. Drink less than you would at home.
  • Stay in Control: Your judgment is your most important safety tool. Never drink to the point where you lose awareness of your surroundings or your ability to make smart decisions.
  • Alternate with Water: For every alcoholic beverage you have, drink a full glass of water. This keeps you hydrated and slows down your alcohol consumption.

Never Accept Drinks from Strangers

Accepting drinks from strangers, no matter how friendly they seem, puts you at serious risk of drink spiking.

  • Buy Your Own Drinks: Make it a strict rule to always buy your own drinks and watch the bartender prepare them from start to finish.
  • Be Wary of Overly Insistent Offers: While some people are genuinely friendly, be cautious of anyone who is aggressively insistent on buying you a drink, especially if you've just met.
  • Recognize the Danger: Drink spiking is a real and common crime used to facilitate robbery or sexual assault. This rule is non-negotiable.

Keep Your Drink With You Always

Leaving your drink unattended, even for a moment, gives someone an opportunity to spike it with drugs or other substances.

  • The Bathroom Rule: If you need to use the restroom, either finish your drink before you go or abandon it and buy a new one when you return.
  • On the Dance Floor: Don't leave your drink at a table while you dance. Take it with you or go without.
  • Trust No One (with your drink): Do not even ask a new acquaintance to watch your drink for you. It's not worth the risk.

Know Your Alcohol Limits

Drinking too much makes you an easy target and puts you in dangerous situations you can't handle properly.

  • Understand Affecting Factors: Travel fatigue, dehydration, and altitude can all dramatically lower your alcohol tolerance. One drink might feel like two.
  • Set a Hard Limit: Before you start drinking, decide on a maximum number of drinks for the evening and stick to it.
  • Emergency Planning: Before you have your first drink, ensure you have a solid plan for getting back to your accommodation safely, including having enough cash for a taxi.

Stay Hydrated with Water

Proper hydration is especially critical when traveling, as dehydration affects judgment, energy levels, and physical ability.

  • Drink Proactively: Don't wait until you feel thirsty. Sip water consistently throughout the day, especially in hot climates or at high altitudes.
  • Monitor Yourself: A good indicator of hydration is your urine color. It should be light yellow. If it's dark, you need to drink more water immediately.
  • Carry Your Own Bottle: Bring a reusable water bottle and fill it with purified or bottled water. This ensures you always have safe water on hand.
