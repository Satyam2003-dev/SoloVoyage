Social Safety & Meeting People

Making new friends is one of the best parts of solo travel. These tips will help you build connections while keeping yourself safe.

Meet New People in Public Places

Your safety must come first when meeting someone new. Always choose a public, populated, and neutral location for first meetings.

  • Safe Meeting Spots: Opt for busy coffee shops, popular restaurants, hotel lobbies, or public parks during daytime hours. These places have witnesses and staff who can help if needed.
  • Locations to Avoid: Never agree to meet someone for the first time at their home, your accommodation, or any secluded, private location. Decline any suggestions to move to a "quieter" spot.
  • Maintain Independence: Arrive and leave using your own transportation. Do not depend on a new acquaintance for a ride, as this gives them control over your movement.

Tell Someone When Meeting New People

Before you meet someone new, inform a trusted contact of your plans. This person is your safety check-in.

  • Share Key Details: Tell your contact the name of the person you're meeting, where you are going, and what time you plan to be back. If possible, share a photo of the person from their dating profile or social media.
  • Establish a Check-in Time: Arrange a specific time to text or call your contact after the meeting is over to let them know you're safe.
  • Have a Backup Plan: Agree on what your contact should do if you do not check in as planned (e.g., wait 30 minutes, then call your hotel, then contact local authorities).

Trust Your Instincts About People

If someone makes you uncomfortable, that's all the reason you need to remove yourself from the situation. Don't second-guess your gut feeling.

  • Red Flags: Be wary of people who ask too many personal questions too quickly (about money, where you're staying), ignore your boundaries, or try to isolate you from others.
  • Don't Worry About Being "Nice": Many people, especially women, are conditioned to be polite. Your safety is more important than someone else's feelings. It is okay to be rude to be safe.
  • Create an Exit: If you feel uneasy, you don't need a dramatic excuse. Simply say, "It was nice meeting you, but I have to go now," and leave confidently.

Don't Give Personal Information Too Quickly

Keep some details private until you know someone better and have established trust over time.

  • Protected Information: Your exact accommodation address, room number, full travel itinerary, and financial situation are private. Do not share them freely.
  • Be Vague: When asked where you're staying, you can name the neighborhood rather than the specific hotel. If asked about your plans, you can speak in general terms.
  • Build Trust Gradually: As you get to know someone over several meetings in public places, you can decide if you feel comfortable sharing more specific details.

Join Group Tours or Activities

Group activities are a fantastic and inherently safer way to meet other travelers and locals than one-on-one encounters.

  • Safety in Numbers: Being in a group provides a natural safety net. You're surrounded by other people and usually led by a professional local guide.
  • Structured Environment: Activities like city walking tours, cooking classes, or day trips are structured, reducing the chance of ending up in an unsafe situation.
  • Observe Social Dynamics: A group setting allows you to observe how people behave before you consider spending time with them individually.

Meet Dates in Public Places

Never go to someone's home on first meetings, regardless of how well you think you know them from online interactions.

  • First Date Rule: The first date should always be in a busy, public place, ideally during the daytime. A coffee date is a perfect, low-pressure option.
  • No Private Locations: Do not let a date persuade you to go to their apartment, a secluded park, or any private location. If they are pushy about this, it is a major red flag.
  • Arrange Your Own Transport: Always have your own way to get to and from the date. Do not rely on them for a ride home.

Let Someone Know Your Dating Plans

Tell a friend or family member where you're going when meeting someone for romantic purposes. This is a non-negotiable safety step.

  • Share All Details: Provide your contact with the person's name, a screenshot of their profile, the exact location of the date, and the time you're meeting.
  • Live Location Sharing: For an extra layer of safety, consider sharing your live GPS location with your trusted contact for the duration of the date.
  • Post-Date Check-in: Always remember to text your contact when you have safely returned to your accommodation after the date.

Have Your Own Transportation

Don't depend on dates or new friends to get you home safely. Maintaining control over your own transportation is essential.

  • Transportation Independence: Research public transport options to and from meeting locations, and have trusted local taxi or ride-sharing apps ready on your phone.
  • Decline Rides: Politely decline offers for a ride home from someone you've just met. This maintains your independence and prevents you from being in a vulnerable situation.
  • Have Emergency Cash: Always keep enough cash hidden away specifically for an emergency taxi ride home, just in case your phone dies or apps aren't working.

Video Chat Before Meeting in Person

A quick video call can help you verify a person's identity and get a feel for their personality before committing to a face-to-face meeting.

  • Verify Identity: A video chat confirms that the person matches their online photos and is not a "catfish."
  • Gauge Comfort Level: This is a great way to assess their communication style and your comfort level with them. You can learn a lot from a 5-minute conversation.
  • Red Flag Alert: If a person is hesitant or makes excuses to avoid a video call, be very cautious. This is often a red flag.

Meet During Daytime First

Evening meetings can wait until you know someone better and have established a foundation of trust.

  • Daylight Advantage: Daytime meetings are inherently safer due to better visibility and more people being around. They also tend to be more casual and less pressure-filled.
  • Build Trust: Use one or two successful, comfortable daytime meetings as a prerequisite before even considering an evening meeting.
  • Public and Well-Lit: If you do transition to an evening meeting, ensure it is in a very public, well-lit, and reputable location that you are familiar with.
