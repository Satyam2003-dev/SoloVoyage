Emergency Preparedness & Response

Nobody plans for an emergency, but every smart traveler prepares for one. Knowing how to react can turn a disaster into a manageable situation.

Stay Calm in Emergencies

Panicking makes situations worse and impairs your ability to think clearly and make good decisions. Your mind is your primary tool.

  • Breathe First: Before you do anything else, take a few slow, deep breaths. This simple action slows your heart rate and helps you think more clearly.
  • Assess, Don't Assume: Take a moment to assess the situation. What is the immediate threat? What is the first, most logical step to improve your safety?
  • Focus on What You Can Control: You can't control a natural disaster or a flight cancellation, but you can control your reaction. Focus on small, actionable steps.

Know Local Emergency Numbers

Research these before you travel and have them easily accessible. Do not assume it is the same as your home country.

  • Save Them Everywhere: Program the numbers for police, ambulance, and fire into your phone. Also, write them on a piece of paper in your wallet and money belt.
  • Universal Numbers: While 112 is a common emergency number in many countries (especially in Europe), and 911 in North America, this is not universal. Confirm the correct numbers for your specific destination.
  • Include Embassy Info: Also save the 24/7 emergency contact number for your country's nearest embassy or consulate.

Find Your Nearest Embassy or Consulate

They are your government's presence abroad and can provide critical assistance in serious situations like a lost passport or an unjust arrest.

  • Know Before You Go: Before your trip, look up the address, phone number, and operating hours of your country's embassy or consulate in your destination.
  • What They Can Do: Embassies can help replace a lost or stolen passport, provide a list of local doctors and lawyers, and offer assistance in major emergencies like natural disasters.
  • Register Your Trip: Use your government's traveler registration program (like the US's STEP program). This allows the embassy to find and assist you in a large-scale emergency.

Have Emergency Contacts in Your Phone (ICE)

First responders are trained to look for "ICE" (In Case of Emergency) contacts in a person's phone if they are unable to communicate.

  • Set Up ICE Contacts: Most smartphones have a Medical ID or Emergency Contact feature that is accessible even when the phone is locked. Set this up with your primary contacts.
  • Include Key Info: In the medical notes section, include your blood type, any allergies, and current medications. This information can be life-saving.
  • Have a Physical Backup: Keep a card in your wallet with this same information, just in case something happens to your phone.

Keep Emergency Cash Hidden

If your wallet and cards are lost or stolen, a hidden stash of emergency cash will be your lifeline to get to safety.

  • The Stash: Hide at least $100 USD (or a similar amount in a major currency) somewhere completely separate from your wallet and money belt, such as deep inside your luggage.
  • Purpose of the Stash: This money is not for souvenirs. It is strictly for a true emergency, like paying for a taxi to your embassy or a last-minute hotel room.
  • Multiple Locations: For longer trips, consider splitting your emergency cash between two different hidden locations for added security.

Go to Busy Public Places if You Feel Unsafe

If you feel you are being followed or are in a situation that makes you uneasy, a public space with other people is your immediate safe haven.

  • Seek Out Staff: Walk confidently into a hotel lobby, a busy restaurant, or a large shop. Approach a staff member and calmly explain that you feel unsafe and need help.
  • Witnesses are a Deterrent: Criminals are less likely to act when there are many witnesses. The presence of other people is a powerful form of protection.
  • Don't Lead Them to Your Room: Go to a neutral public space, not back to your own hotel, as you don't want a potential threat to know where you are staying.

Ask for Help from Authorities

Police, hotel staff, or shop owners can assist you when you're in trouble or feeling unsafe. Don't be afraid to ask.

  • Identify Helpers: Look for uniformed police or security, hotel front desk staff, or the manager of a store or restaurant.
  • Be Clear and Calm: Approach them and calmly state your situation. For example, "I am being followed, can you please call the police for me?"
  • Use Gestures if Needed: If there is a language barrier, don't give up. Use gestures and a firm, serious tone to convey that you are in distress and need assistance.

Call Someone You Trust

If you find yourself in a scary or confusing situation, call a trusted friend or family member back home.

  • A Calming Voice: Just hearing a familiar voice can help you calm down and think more clearly.
  • A Second Brain: Explain your situation to them. They are outside the stressful environment and may be able to think of a solution or look up information for you that you're too panicked to find.
  • Inform and Document: At a minimum, you are letting someone know your exact location and what is happening in real-time, which is a critical safety step.

Don't Be Afraid to Make Noise

Yell for help if you need it. Attracting attention is a highly effective way to deter attackers and summon assistance.

  • Be Loud and Specific: Yell "FIRE!" instead of "Help!" as it often gets a faster response. Use a whistle or personal alarm if you have one.
  • Overcome Politeness: Do not worry about causing a scene or being embarrassed. Your safety is infinitely more important than a moment of public awkwardness.
  • Draw Attention to the Attacker: If possible, point directly at the person threatening you and yell, "This person is trying to rob me! Call the police!"

Remove Yourself from Dangerous Situations

Your safety is more important than being polite or not wanting to offend others. If a situation feels wrong, get out.

  • Immediate Action: The moment you feel your safety is compromised, your only goal is to leave. You do not owe anyone an explanation or an apology.
  • Have an Excuse Ready: If you need a "polite" way out, have an excuse ready, such as "I'm sorry, I just got a text and I have to leave immediately."
  • Trust Your Gut, Always: This is the core principle. If your intuition is screaming at you, listen. It is always better to be rude and safe than polite and a victim.

The Ultimate Solo Travel Safety Principle: Trust Yourself and Stay Alert

You know when something doesn't feel right. Your intuition is your most powerful safety tool. When in doubt, trust your gut and prioritize your safety above all else.

  • Listen to Your Body: Pay attention to physical reactions—the hairs on your neck standing up, a knot in your stomach. These are subconscious signals of danger.
  • Vigilance is Not Fear: Staying alert isn't about being paranoid; it's about being present and aware. It's what allows you to relax, knowing you are prepared and in control.
  • Your Safety is Your Responsibility: No photo, no adventure, no social obligation is worth compromising your well-being. The ultimate goal of travel is to return home safely with amazing memories.
