Digital & Online Safety

In today's world, your digital footprint is as important as your physical one. Protect your data and online presence with these essential tips.

Download Safety Apps Before Travel

Technology can provide crucial assistance in emergency situations. Set up these apps before you leave home.

  • Essential Apps: Download local ride-sharing apps (like Uber, Lyft, or a local equivalent), offline maps (Google Maps), translation apps (Google Translate), and any personal safety apps that can alert emergency contacts.
  • Personal Safety Features: Look for apps with features like a panic button that alerts contacts with your GPS location, automatic check-in reminders, or a loud alarm.
  • Test Everything: Ensure the apps work on your phone, you understand how to use all the features, and you've pre-loaded them with your emergency contact information.

Use a VPN on Public Wi-Fi

Public Wi-Fi networks in cafes, airports, and hotels are often unsecured and can expose your personal information to hackers. A VPN is your digital bodyguard.

  • How it Works: A VPN (Virtual Private Network) encrypts your internet connection, creating a secure tunnel that hides your browsing activity, passwords, and financial data from anyone snooping on the network.
  • Choose Reputable Services: Avoid free VPNs, which may sell your data. Opt for well-known, paid services that have strong privacy policies and servers in multiple countries.
  • Best Practices: Always connect to your VPN *before* you access any public Wi-Fi. Even with a VPN, avoid logging into highly sensitive accounts like your primary bank account on public networks.

Don't Share Exact Locations on Social Media

Broadcasting your location in real-time makes you vulnerable to theft, stalking, and other crimes, both abroad and at home.

  • Post with a Delay: The golden rule is to post about a location *after* you have left it. Share that amazing cafe photo when you're back at your hotel, not while you're still there.
  • Be Vague: Use general location tags (e.g., "Paris, France") instead of checking in at specific hotels, restaurants, or attractions. Never post a picture of your hotel room key or any document with your name and address.
  • Protect Your Home: Posting in real-time also announces to burglars back home that your house is empty. Save the full photo album for when you return.

Keep Your Phone Charged

A dead phone can leave you unable to call for help, navigate, or access important information. It is your most critical piece of safety equipment.

  • Carry a Power Bank: Always carry a fully charged portable battery pack with enough capacity to charge your phone at least twice.
  • Conserve Battery Life: Use power-saving modes, reduce screen brightness, and close apps you aren't using to extend your battery's life throughout the day.
  • Have a Backup Plan: Have important phone numbers (emergency services, your hotel, your embassy) written down on a piece of paper in case your phone dies completely.

Save Important Phone Numbers

Don't rely solely on internet access or cloud storage for critical contact information. Save them directly to your phone and on paper.

  • Essential Numbers: Save local emergency services (police, ambulance), your embassy's 24/7 line, your accommodation's contact info, your bank's fraud department, and your top emergency contact at home.
  • ICE Contacts: Create an "In Case of Emergency" (ICE) contact in your phone. First responders are trained to look for this if you are incapacitated.
  • Physical Backup: Write down your most critical numbers on a small card and keep it in your wallet or money belt, separate from your phone.

Don't Post That You're Traveling Alone

Revealing that you're traveling solo on a public platform can make you a target for criminals and others with bad intentions.

  • Imply Companionship: Use plural pronouns in your captions, such as "We are having a great time!" or "What a view we saw today!"
  • Privacy Settings: Make your social media profiles private before your trip so only trusted friends and family can see your posts.
  • Be Wary of New "Friends": Be cautious about accepting friend requests from strangers you meet during your travels until you know them well. They could be trying to gather information about you.

Turn Off Location Services for Social Apps

Many apps track and share your location without you realizing it, often embedding your precise GPS coordinates into the photos you post.

  • Check Your Permissions: Go into your phone's settings and review which apps have access to your location. Turn off location access for social media apps like Instagram, Facebook, and Twitter.
  • Scrub Photo Data: Be aware that photos can contain EXIF data, which includes the location, date, and time a photo was taken. You can use apps to scrub this data before posting if you're concerned.
  • Essential Apps Only: Keep location services on for essential apps like maps for navigation or "Find My Phone," but disable it for everything else.

Be Careful About New Friend Requests

Criminals often use social media to gather information about potential victims before making a move.

  • Vet New Requests: Don't immediately accept friend requests from people you've just met. Take some time to get to know them in person first.
  • Look for Red Flags: Be suspicious of profiles with very few friends, no photos, or that were created very recently.
  • Protect Your Information: Even after you accept a request, be mindful of what you share. Avoid posting details that could compromise your safety.

Don't Share Travel Plans Online

Publicly posting detailed travel information gives criminals a roadmap to target you or your home.

  • Avoid Specifics: Never post photos of your boarding pass, your full itinerary, or the exact dates you will be away.
  • Be Mindful of Your Audience: Even if your profile is private, be aware that your friends' friends might see your posts. Keep sensitive details out of public view.
  • Share Privately: Use private messaging, email, or a small, trusted group chat to share detailed updates with close family and friends.

Use Private Messaging Instead of Public Posts

Keep your travel communications private to maintain complete control over who has access to your information.

  • Control Your Information: Public posts can be screenshotted and shared beyond your control. Private messages keep your conversations contained.
  • Share More Safely: You can share more detailed information, like your specific hotel or your flight number, with a trusted family member via a secure, private message.
  • Use Encrypted Apps: For maximum security, use end-to-end encrypted messaging apps like Signal or WhatsApp for sensitive communications.
