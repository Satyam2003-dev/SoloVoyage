Money & Valuables Safety

Smart financial habits and a bit of caution can ensure your valuables stay safe, letting you focus on the fun.

Use ATMs Inside Banks or Hotels

Indoor ATMs are safer than standalone street machines that are easier for criminals to tamper with or monitor.

  • Preferred Locations: The safest choice is an ATM inside a bank branch during business hours. Hotel lobbies, major shopping malls, and airports are also good options.
  • Physical Inspection: Before using an ATM, check for suspicious devices. Wiggle the card reader and keypadâ€”if anything feels loose or out of place, do not use it.
  • Shield Your PIN: Always cover the keypad with your other hand when entering your PIN. This simple action blocks both prying eyes and hidden cameras.

Don't Count Money in Public

Handling large amounts of cash in public attracts unwanted attention and identifies you as a potential target for theft.

  • Count in Private: After withdrawing cash, step into a private space like a bathroom stall or wait until you are back in your locked hotel room to count it.
  • Organize for the Day: Only carry the cash you'll need for the day in your wallet. Keep the rest secured in your money belt or hotel safe.
  • Avoid Flashing Bills: When making a purchase, try to have small bills ready so you don't have to pull out a large wad of cash, revealing how much you're carrying.

Carry Small Bills

Having small denominations prevents you from flashing large bills and makes transactions smoother, especially with small vendors.

  • Easier Transactions: Small bills are perfect for tips, street food, market purchases, and public transport, where vendors may not have change for large notes.
  • Less Attractive to Thieves: A pickpocket is more interested in a wallet full of large bills than one with small change.
  • Obtaining Small Bills: When exchanging money at a bank, specifically ask for a mix of denominations, including plenty of small notes.

Have a Backup Card Hidden Separately

If your primary cards are lost, stolen, or compromised, a backup card will prevent you from being stranded without access to money.

  • Diversify Your Cards: Carry at least one credit card and one debit card from different banks (e.g., a Visa and a Mastercard). If one network has an issue, the other may still work.
  • Store Separately: Never keep all your cards in the same wallet. Keep your primary card in your wallet and your backup cards secured in your money belt or hotel safe.
  • Inform Your Banks: Remember to notify all your banks about your travel plans for every card you bring with you.

Divide Your Money Between Multiple Places

Never carry all your money in one location. If that single spot is compromised, you'll be left with nothing.

  • The 3-Stash Rule: A good system is to have: 1) daily spending money in your wallet, 2) the bulk of your cash in your hidden money belt, and 3) an emergency stash hidden deep in your luggage or in the hotel safe.
  • Creative Hiding Spots: Consider hiding emergency cash in unlikely places like an empty chapstick tube, inside a shoe, or sewn into the lining of a bag.
  • Currency Diversification: Your emergency stash should ideally include some of your home currency or a widely accepted currency like US Dollars or Euros, in addition to local currency.

Use Crossbody Bags with Zippers

The design of your bag can make a huge difference in deterring theft. A crossbody bag is significantly safer than a shoulder bag or backpack.

  • Secure by Design: A crossbody strap makes it very difficult for someone to snatch your bag. Always wear the strap over your head, not just on your shoulder.
  • Zippers are Non-Negotiable: Choose bags with sturdy zippers. Open-top tote bags or those with simple magnetic clasps are an open invitation for pickpockets.
  • Keep it in Front: In crowded places like markets or subways, swing your bag to the front of your body and keep your hand resting on it.

Keep Bags Closed and in Front in Crowds

Crowded areas provide the perfect cover for pickpockets and bag-snatchers to operate without being noticed.

  • Body-Blocking: In crowded transport or markets, wear your backpack on your front. Keep a crossbody bag tight against the front of your body with your arm over it.
  • Be Wary of Distractions: A common tactic is for one person to "accidentally" bump into you or ask for directions while an accomplice picks your pocket or opens your bag. Be aware of your personal space.
  • Zipper Checks: Make it a habit to subconsciously check that your zippers are closed, especially after moving through a dense crowd.

Don't Put Valuables in Back Pockets

Back pockets are the easiest targets for pickpockets and items are more likely to fall out without you noticing.

  • Front Pockets Only: Your phone and wallet should only ever go in your front pockets, which are much harder for a thief to access without you noticing.
  • Inside Pockets are Even Better: An inside jacket pocket is one of the most secure places to carry a phone or a thin wallet.
  • Develop the Habit: Make it an unbreakable habit to never use your back pockets for anything valuable, both at home and abroad.

Use Travel Locks on Luggage

While a determined thief can get through any lock, a simple lock is a powerful deterrent against opportunistic theft.

  • Deter Casual Theft: Locks prevent someone from quickly and discreetly opening your bag in a luggage hold, on a train, or in a hostel room.
  • TSA-Approved Locks: Use TSA-approved locks on checked baggage. This allows security personnel to open your bag with a master key if needed, without cutting your lock off.
  • Secure to Fixtures: Consider a cable lock, which allows you to secure your bag to a fixed object like a luggage rack on a train or a bedpost in a hostel.

Take Photos of Your Belongings

Having photographic documentation of your valuables is invaluable for police reports and insurance claims if they are stolen.

  • Photograph Everything: Before your trip, take clear photos of your expensive electronics (including serial numbers), jewelry, and even your luggage.
  • Cloud Storage: Upload these photos to a secure cloud service like Google Drive, iCloud, or Dropbox so you can access them from anywhere, even if your phone is stolen.
  • Keep Receipts: For very expensive items, also keep digital copies of the receipts. This makes proving the value of the item to an insurance company much easier.
