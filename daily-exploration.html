Daily Exploration Safety

This is where the adventure happens! Staying aware and making smart choices on the street is the key to a successful day of exploring.

Stay in Well-Lit, Busy Areas

Criminals prefer isolated areas where they won't be seen or interrupted. Your best defense is to stay where the people are.

  • Plan Your Routes: Especially at night, plan your walking route to stick to main roads with plenty of streetlights, shops, and pedestrian traffic.
  • Avoid Shortcuts: Never take shortcuts through dark alleys, empty parks, or unlit side streets, no matter how much time it might save.
  • Have an Escape Plan: If you accidentally wander into an area that feels deserted or unsafe, turn around immediately and walk quickly back to a busy, public space.

Walk with Confidence

Confident body language makes you a less likely target. Predators look for people who seem lost, distracted, or vulnerable.

  • Posture and Pace: Walk with your head up, shoulders back, and at a steady, purposeful pace. Make brief eye contact with people you pass.
  • Look Like You Belong: Try to blend in. Avoid wearing flashy tourist clothing or expensive items that scream "I'm not from here."
  • Consult Maps Discreetly: If you're lost, step into a shop or cafe to check your map or phone. Avoid looking confused on a public street corner.

Keep Your Phone Visible but Secure

Your phone is essential for safety but also valuable to thieves. Handle it with care.

  • Secure Your Grip: When using your phone on the street, hold it firmly with both hands. Consider a phone case with a wrist strap or a PopSocket for a better grip.
  • Be Aware of Snatchers: Be especially cautious near roads where thieves on scooters can easily snatch a phone and speed away. Step away from the curb when using your phone.
  • Position Yourself Defensively: When stationary and using your phone, stand with your back to a wall so no one can approach you from behind.

Trust Your Gut Feelings

Your intuition often recognizes danger before your conscious mind does. This is your most powerful safety tool.

  • Listen to Unease: If a person, place, or situation makes you feel uncomfortable for any reason, listen to that feeling. You don't need to justify it.
  • Prioritize Safety Over Politeness: Do not worry about being rude or hurting someone's feelings. If your gut says "leave," you leave immediately.
  • Act Decisively: Remove yourself from the situation. Cross the street, enter a store, or call a taxi. Your safety is paramount.

Don't Wear Headphones in Unfamiliar Places

Headphones block important audio cues from your environment and signal to others that you are not paying attention.

  • Maintain Auditory Awareness: You need to be able to hear approaching footsteps, vehicles, or shouts for help. Your ears are a critical part of your safety system.
  • One Earbud Rule: If you must listen to something, use only one earbud and keep the volume low enough to hear your surroundings.
  • Situational Ban: Never wear headphones when walking alone at night, in crowded markets, or on public transportation.

Research Public Transportation Routes

Understanding local transportation systems before you need them prevents you from getting lost or stranded in unsafe areas.

  • Study the Map: Before your trip, download transit apps and study the metro or bus maps. Know the names of the stops near your accommodation and key attractions.
  • Identify Safe Routes: Research which lines or routes are considered safe, especially after dark. Avoid routes that pass through known high-crime areas.
  • Have a Backup Plan: Know what your alternative transportation options are (e.g., taxi, ride-sharing) if the public transit system closes for the night or service is disrupted.

Keep Your Bag in Front on Crowded Transport

Pickpockets thrive in crowded buses and trains where physical contact is common and expected.

  • Backpack on Front: If you are wearing a backpack, swing it around to your chest. This keeps all your zippers in your line of sight.
  • Secure Your Handbag: For shoulder or crossbody bags, keep them zipped and positioned in front of your body with your hand or arm resting securely over the top.
  • Be Wary of Distractions: Pickpockets often work in teams. One might create a commotion or "accidentally" bump into you while another steals your wallet. Be aware of your surroundings.

Sit Near the Driver or Conductor

On public transportation like buses or trains, transportation staff can provide assistance and their presence deters criminal activity.

  • Choose Strategic Seating: Sit as close to the driver as possible, in a well-lit area of the vehicle with a clear view of the exits.
  • Avoid Isolated Areas: Do not sit in empty train cars or the back of a bus, especially late at night. You are more vulnerable in these locations.
  • Stay Alert: Even when seated, keep your bag in your lap or on the floor between your feet, and remain aware of who gets on and off.

Have Transportation Tickets Ready

Fumbling for tickets or money at a turnstile or when boarding a bus makes you look like a confused tourist and exposes your wallet.

  • Prepare in Advance: Have your ticket, pass, or exact change in your hand or an easily accessible pocket *before* you reach the payment point.
  • Separate Transit Money: Keep money for transportation in a separate, easily accessible pocket to avoid pulling out your main wallet full of cash and cards.
  • Observe Locals: Watch how local people pay and board to understand the process before it's your turn.

Pay Attention to Your Stop

Missing your stop can leave you in an unfamiliar or potentially unsafe area, especially at night.

  • Follow Along on a Map: Use a map app on your phone (like Google Maps with GPS) to follow your journey in real-time so you know when your stop is approaching.
  • Know the Stop Before Yours: Identify the name of the stop right before your destination. When you hear or see it, you'll know it's time to get ready.
  • Don't Be Afraid to Ask: If you're unsure, ask the driver or a fellow passenger to let you know when you're approaching a specific stop. Most people are happy to help.
